Networking Basics
Definition · Classification · Inter-Networking · Network Devices
Topic 1
1.1 Definition of Networking

Computer Network is the actual setup of Networking. Networking is the process of setting up a network for the purpose of resource sharing — i.e. Networking is a group of computers or devices coming together to share resources.

🗣️ Plain English
Imagine your school has 50 computers. Instead of having a USB drive on every single desk, networking connects them all so they can share the same printer, the same files, and the same internet connection. That's what networking is — making devices talk to each other to share things.

Based on Network Model:

🔄 P2P (Peer-to-Peer)

All computers are equal. Every device can both send and receive. No central boss. Example: BitTorrent, WhatsApp calls.

🖥️ Client-Server

One powerful computer (server) serves many weaker ones (clients). Example: When you visit google.com, your browser is the client, Google's machine is the server.

1.3 Inter-Networking

Inter-networking is the process of connecting multiple independent networks to form a larger unified network system, i.e. networking on a much larger scale. It is the foundation of the internet that allows diverse network such as connection of LAN, MAN and WAN connected through ISPs.

🗣️ Plain English
Think of each campus, company, or country having its own private network. Inter-networking is the bridge that connects all those separate networks together. The Internet is the biggest example of inter-networking on earth — it links millions of individual networks through ISPs (Internet Service Providers).

Examples of Inter-networking: Internet  |  Cloud Computing  |  Corporate Intranet

OSI & TCP/IP Models
Layers · Functions · Groups · Comparison
Topic 2
2.1 OSI (Open System Interconnection)

OSI is an open system interconnection model developed by international standards. It has 7 layers. Click any layer to see its function.

7
Application
Software
Closest to the user. This is the layer you interact with — your browser, email app, or FTP client. Protocols: HTTP, HTTPS, FTP, SMTP, DNS.
6
Presentation
Software
Translates data formats, handles encryption/decryption and compression. Like a translator between the application and the network. Example: SSL/TLS encryption happens here.
5
Session
Software
Manages sessions (conversations) between applications. Opens, maintains, and closes communication sessions. Like the "start call / end call" logic on a phone.
4
Transport
Software
Ensures end-to-end delivery of data. Breaks data into segments. Provides error recovery and flow control. Protocols: TCP (reliable), UDP (fast but no guarantee).
3
Network
Hardware
Handles logical addressing (IP addresses) and routing between networks. Routers operate here. Determines the best path from source to destination across multiple networks.
2
Data Link
Hardware
Handles MAC addressing and error detection within a local network. Switches operate here. Packages raw bits into frames and ensures reliable transmission between adjacent nodes.
1
Physical
Hardware
The actual physical cables, radio waves, and electrical signals. Deals with voltage levels, cable types (fiber, copper), and transmission media. This is the wire and the plug.
🗣️ Memory Trick
"All People Seem To Need Data Processing" — Application, Presentation, Session, Transport, Network, Data Link, Physical (Layer 7 to 1).
💡 Real-Life Analogy
Sending an email is like sending a letter by courier: you write it (Application), it gets translated into a standard format (Presentation), a session is opened (Session), the letter is packaged (Transport), routed through post offices (Network), put on a van (Data Link), and physically delivered on a road (Physical).
2.2 TCP/IP Model

TCP/IP are models that describe how computers communicate over a network. It has 4 layers and is what the actual Internet runs on.

Layer #NameCorresponds to OSIExample Protocols
4ApplicationApplication + Presentation + SessionHTTP, FTP, DNS, SMTP
3TransportTransportTCP, UDP
2InternetNetworkIP, ICMP
1Network AccessData Link + PhysicalEthernet, Wi-Fi
✅ OSI vs TCP/IP Summary
OSI is a conceptual/reference model (7 layers, used for teaching and understanding). TCP/IP is the practical model (4 layers, what the Internet actually uses). You study OSI to understand how networks work; TCP/IP is the real implementation.

Net-Centric Computing
Definition · Characteristics · Diverse Applications
Topic 3
3.1 What is Net-Centric Computing?

Net-Centric Computing is a model or a standard where computing resources, data and application are accessed and managed through network rather than locally. It allows multiple system to collaborate on a single large task, leveraging on the capabilities of the connected devices.

It allows system to be scaleable, fault tolerant and secure, allowing users from different locations to access shared resources in real time.

🗣️ Plain English
Before Net-Centric Computing, everything had to be on your computer — your software, your files, your processing power. Net-Centric flips this: instead of everything being local, it all lives on the network. Your device just becomes a window to access it. Think: Google Docs vs Microsoft Word installed on your PC. Google Docs is Net-Centric.
3.2 Diverse Applications of Net-Centric Computing
#ApplicationDefinitionExample
1Cloud ComputingAccess to computing resources over the internetGoogle Drive, SharePoint
2IoT (Internet of Things)Network of physical devices connected to the internet for sharing data; capable of collecting, sharing and receiving data for better automationSmart home devices, fitness trackers
3Big Data AnalyticsProcesses and analyzes large data sets that traditional computers cannot handleGoogle Colab, TensorFlow, Hadoop
4Collaborative ToolsSoftware that allows multiple users to work together in real timeFigma, Discord, GitHub
5E-Commerce & Social NetworksThe underlined technology for Net-Centric Computing is referred to as Distributed SystemAmazon, Instagram, Twitter
💡 Key Takeaway
The underlined (underlying) technology that powers all of Net-Centric Computing is the Distributed System. Everything from cloud computing to social media is built on distributed system principles.

Distributed Systems
Definition · Modes · Architecture (Client-Server, N-Tier, P2P)
Topic 4
4.1 What are Distributed Systems?

A Distributed System involves multiple autonomous computers called nodes working over a network to appear as a single system computer to the users. The nodes can be physically connected or geographically separated.

It is essentially teams or groups of network computers working together to appear as a single system.

🗣️ Plain English
When you search on Google, you think you're talking to "Google" — one computer. In reality, thousands of servers across the world are working together simultaneously to answer your query. That's a distributed system. Many machines, one experience.
4.2 Modes of Distributed System Operation
1. Collaboratively

All the computers work together on a single task and the end users see the result as if it came from a single computer.

🔎 Example: Google's search engine — hundreds of servers process your query together and give you ONE results page.

2. Independently

Each computer has its own user or task but the system will allow them to share resources.

🔎 Example: AAUA computer lab — each student has their own session, but they all share the same printer and internet connection.

4.3 Architecture of Distributed System
1️⃣ Client-Server▼

One computer provides the data or services and that computer is known as the server while the other computer requests the data and that is the client.

🗣️ Real Life
Going to a restaurant: you (client) sit at the table and order food. The kitchen (server) prepares and serves it. You only make requests; the kitchen does the work.
2️⃣ Three Tier / Layer▼

An additional layer is added between the client and the server. The additional layer known as the middle layer handles the logic and data storage so that the client does not need to manage anything.

💡 Example
ATM Machine: You (client) → ATM software (middle layer handling logic) → Bank's database (server). The ATM logic sits in the middle so the bank doesn't expose its full database to your card.
3️⃣ N-Tier▼

This extends farther than a three tier model — requests pass through multiple layers of services with each layer performing a specialized function.

✅ Example
A large e-commerce site: Browser (client) → Load Balancer → Web Server → Application Server → Payment Service → Notification Service → Database. Each tier does one job.
4️⃣ P2P (Peer to Peer)▼

All computers share the same responsibility, acting both as the client and the server.

💡 Example
BitTorrent: when you download a movie, you're simultaneously downloading it FROM other users AND uploading pieces of it TO others. You are both client and server.

Everything as a Service (XaaS)
IaaS · PaaS · SaaS · FaaS · BaaS · CaaS — tap each to expand
Topic 5
5.1 What is XaaS?

XaaS (Everything as a Service) is the practical implementation of Net-Centric Computing and Distributed Systems. The cloud implementation of service orientation is referred to as XaaS — which means treating IT resources into services that can be used and consumed over the internet.

💡 Key Principle
XaaS is the service layer of Distributed Systems. It talks about how distributed resources are abstracted and delivered over networks. Net-Centric Computing is the idea; XaaS is the practical delivery.
5.2 The Six XaaS Models — Click to expand each
🏗️
IaaS — Infrastructure as a Service
Basic raw computing resources over the cloud ▾

Definition: IaaS provides basic computing resources over the cloud, such as virtual servers, storage, and networks. The provider manages the physical hardware; the user manages software, applications, and data.

🗣️ Plain English: Renting an empty warehouse. The building is theirs, but you decide what furniture and boxes go inside. You have full control over what you put in it.

💡 Examples: Amazon EC2, Microsoft Azure Virtual Machines, Google Compute Engine.

Hardware accessed over cloud: Storage, Virtual Drives, Networks.

🔧
PaaS — Platform as a Service
Ready-to-use platform for building apps ▾

Definition: PaaS provides a ready-to-use platform for building, running, and managing applications. The provider handles servers, storage, OS, and runtime environments. The user only writes and deploys code.

🗣️ Plain English: Renting a fully furnished kitchen. The oven, pots, and fridge are there — you just cook. You don't worry about buying or maintaining equipment.

💡 Examples: Google App Engine, Heroku, Microsoft Azure App Service.

The user only handles the hardware requirements — the PaaS handles everything else like servers, storage, OS, and runtime.

💻
SaaS — Software as a Service
Ready-to-use software over the internet ▾

Definition: SaaS provides ready-to-use software applications over the internet. The provider manages the software, updates, and security; the user simply uses it. No installation or maintenance required.

🗣️ Plain English: Ordering food from a restaurant. You don't cook, don't clean, don't buy ingredients — you just eat. Similarly, with SaaS you just use the software.

💡 Examples: Gmail, Google Docs, Zoom, Microsoft 365, Canva.

⚡
FaaS — Function as a Service
Run small code snippets on demand ▾

Definition: FaaS lets you run small pieces of code (functions) on demand, without managing servers. The provider automatically runs the code when triggered. You only pay when the function runs — not for idle time.

Key Idea: "Code runs only when triggered — no server always-on."

🗣️ Plain English: Like a street vendor who only opens their stall when someone walks by. You don't pay rent for a permanent shop — you just sell when there's a customer.

💡 Example: You create a website with a contact form. A small function automatically sends a thank-you email only when someone submits the form — using AWS Lambda.

Other Use Cases: Sending notifications, Processing form submissions.

🗄️
BaaS — Backend as a Service
Ready-made backend infrastructure ▾

Definition: BaaS provides ready-made backend services like databases, authentication, cloud storage, and APIs. The provider handles the backend; the user focuses on frontend or app logic. You don't build backend systems from scratch — you plug into existing services.

🗣️ Plain English: Using IKEA furniture instead of building your own from wood. The pieces are ready-made — you just assemble and use them.

💡 Example: You are building a mobile app and want users to log in with Google accounts. Instead of coding your own login system, you use Firebase Authentication. Instead of building mapping software, you use Google Maps API.

📞
CaaS — Communication as a Service
Cloud-hosted communication tools ▾

Definition: CaaS provides cloud-hosted communication tools like chat, VoIP, and video conferencing. The provider manages the apps and servers; you simply use them online. No installation or server management needed.

🗣️ Plain English: Using a public telephone booth rather than building your own phone network. You just pick up and call.

💡 Example: Your group needs to hold an online project discussion. Instead of setting up your own chat or video system, you use Zoom, Microsoft Teams, or Slack.

5.3 Enabling Technologies for Net-Centric Computing
#TechnologyDescription
1Web 2.0Allows users to actively engage in services over the internet using interactive interfaces (e.g. social media, wikis)
2SOA (Service Oriented Architecture)A design approach where applications are built by combining small independent services that communicate over a network. SOA advocates breaking applications into smaller reusable parts that work together and independently.

SOA Characteristics:

  • Independence: Each service operates independently — can be modified or replaced without affecting others.
  • Reusability: The service must be designed to be used across multiple applications to reduce development cost.
  • Scalability: The service can be scaled up based on demand.

Distributed Systems Interaction Models
Synchronous · Asynchronous · Challenges
Topic 7
7.1 The Two Interaction Models

Architecture tells us "who talks to who." The Interaction Model tells us "when and how they talk."

7.2 Synchronous Distributed System

A synchronous distributed system is one where there is a shared or tightly synchronized clock, a known maximum time for message delivery, and a known maximum time for process execution. In simple words: Time is predictable.

⏱️ SYNCHRONOUS — Time is Predictable
Client
sends
receives ✓
Server
processes
replies ✓
✓ If no reply within known deadline → DEFINITE FAILURE. Time is trustworthy.

Why useful? — Reliable Failure Detection (timeouts are meaningful) and Real-Time Guarantees. Used in: Aircraft control systems, Industrial automation, Medical equipment, Embedded systems.

⚠️ Why it's hard to implement
Real networks are messy — traffic fluctuates, congestion happens, delays vary, packets get lost. Maintaining strict time guarantees across many machines is expensive, complex, and hard to scale. That is why the Internet is generally NOT synchronous.
7.3 Asynchronous Distributed System

In an asynchronous system there is no global clock, message delivery time is unpredictable, and process execution time is unpredictable. In simple terms: Time cannot be trusted.

❓ ASYNCHRONOUS — Time is Unpredictable
Client
sends
waiting... waiting... ??? no reply yet
Server?
crashed? delayed? busy? we don't know...
⚠ Timeout does NOT guarantee failure. We cannot be sure what happened.
🗣️ Plain English
Sending a WhatsApp message to someone who hasn't been seen online. Did they read it and ignore you? Did their phone die? Did your message even deliver? You just don't know — that's asynchronous uncertainty.

Major Challenges in Asynchronous Systems:

ChallengeProblemSolution
Failure DetectionTimeout ≠ failure. Can't be sure if server crashed or message is delayedHeartbeats, Retries, Monitoring systems
Duplicate ExecutionClient retries → same request executed twice (e.g. double payment)Unique request IDs, Idempotent operations, Transaction logs
Event OrderingWithout shared clock: which event happened first?Logical clocks, Event ordering algorithms — track causal order not real time
💡 Key Fact
Cloud platforms (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) mostly operate in an asynchronous model. That is why cloud systems must be carefully designed to handle: Delays, Retries, Failures, and Duplicate messages.

Fault Tolerance in Distributed Systems
Why Failure is Normal · Types of Failure · Techniques
Topic 8
8.1 Why Failure is Normal

In distributed systems, failure is not an exception — it is expected. Unlike a single computer, distributed systems are made up of many computers, many networks, and many software components — so something is always failing somewhere.

🗣️ Plain English
A single car engine rarely breaks mid-trip. But if you had a truck with 500 engines all working together, some would always be failing or getting serviced. Distributed systems are like that — they're designed to keep working even when individual parts break.
8.2 Types of Failures
💥
Crash Failure
Node stops executing completely. No response, no message, completely silent.

Detection: Heartbeat timeout
Example: Server shuts down, VM crashes
📭
Omission Failure
Node fails to send or receive messages. Still alive — just not communicating.

Types: Send omission, Receive omission
Cause: Network congestion, packet drops
⏰
Timing Failure
Response arrives after the deadline. Occurs in synchronous systems.

Critical in: Air traffic control, Medical monitoring, Industrial automation
😈
Byzantine Failure
Node behaves arbitrarily — sends conflicting data, lies, acts maliciously.

Includes: Cyberattacks, Software corruption, Insider threats
8.3 Fault Tolerance Techniques
TechniqueHow it worksFaults it handles
1. ReplicationKeep multiple copies of data, services, and servers. If one fails, others continue.Crash, Omission
2. Consensus AlgorithmsAll nodes agree on a value even when failures exist.Crash, Omission, Timing, Byzantine
3. CheckpointingSystem periodically saves its state. If crash occurs, restore from last checkpoint.Crash
4. Heartbeat MechanismNodes send periodic "I am alive" messages. If heartbeat stops → assume crash.Detects Crash
5. Failover MechanismsAutomatic switching to a backup system when the primary fails — without user noticing.Crash

Mobile Computing & Cloud Threats

Topic 9
9.1 What is Mobile Computing?

Mobile computing is the ability to access and process data while moving across different networks. It extends net-centric computing into wireless environments.

9.2 Components of Mobile Computing

9.3 Challenges in Mobile Computing
  • Limited Battery Power — devices rely on batteries; energy-efficient hardware and software are necessary
  • Unstable Network Bandwidth — signal strength varies due to movement, congestion, or distance from towers
  • Signal Interference — physical obstacles, weather, or other signals reduce connection quality
  • Mobility Management — system must track device location and maintain connection as user moves
  • Increased Security Risks — wireless communication is more exposed to attacks like data interception
9.4 Handoff Mechanism

Handoff is when a mobile device moves from one cell tower to another, the network transfers the connection to the new tower. Needed to maintain: Continuous communication, No dropped calls, No interrupted data sessions.

🗣️ Plain English
Imagine you're on a phone call while driving. Every few kilometers, your signal passes from one phone tower to the next — but you don't notice because the handoff is seamless. That's the handoff mechanism keeping your call alive.
9.5 Mobile Protocols — ZigBee & Z-Wave

These are low-power wireless protocols used mainly in IoT, smart homes, and sensor networks.

💡 Mesh Networking Explained
Devices relay data for each other. No single central hub required. If one device fails, data routes around it through other devices. That is distributed networking in physical form.
9.6 Middleware Technology

Middleware is the glue of distributed systems. It abstracts communication complexity, data format differences, and platform incompatibilities.

Middleware enables: Remote Procedure Calls (RPC), Remote Method Invocation (RMI), REST APIs, Web Services.

✅ Key Point
Without middleware, distributed applications cannot function seamlessly. When a mobile app calls a backend API, that communication is handled by middleware technologies. Middleware is the invisible engine of net-centric computing.

Security in Distributed Systems

Topic 10
10.1 Security Objectives (CIA + AN)
ObjectiveDefinitionExample
ConfidentialitySensitive data is only accessible to authorized users — protected from unauthorized viewingEncryption prevents attackers from reading transmitted data
IntegrityData is not altered or tampered with during storage or transmissionHash functions verify a message has not been modified
AvailabilitySystems and services remain accessible when neededProtection against DDoS attacks keeps services running

Non-RepudiationSender cannot deny sending a message or performing an actionDigital signatures provide proof of origin
10.2 Common Threats
ThreatDefinition
Man-in-the-Middle (MITM)An attacker secretly intercepts and possibly alters communication between two parties without their knowledge
Data InterceptionUnauthorized access to data during transmission over networks
Malware InjectionMalicious software inserted into the system to disrupt operations or steal data
Unauthorized AccessAttackers gain access to systems or data without proper permission
10.3 Security Approaches
ApproachHow it works
Encryption (TLS/SSL)Encrypts data during transmission to prevent interception. Transport Layer Security secures web communication.
Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)Uses digital certificates and asymmetric cryptography to verify identities and secure communication
Access Control Lists (ACLs)Define which users or systems can access specific resources
FirewallsMonitor and filter incoming and outgoing network traffic based on security rules

10.4 Why Distributed Systems Are Harder to Secure
  • More Nodes — Each node (server, device, service) is a potential attack target
  • More Entry Points — Multiple APIs, interfaces, and communication channels increase the attack surface
  • More Communication Paths — Data constantly moves across networks, increasing risk of interception and tampering

Emerging Technologies & Net-Centric Dependency
AI · Blockchain · VR · Drones · Wearables — tap to expand
Topic 11
11.1 How Modern Technologies Depend on Distributed Systems

Every major emerging technology relies on distributed systems and net-centric computing as its backbone. Click any card below to see how.

🤖
Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Definition: Simulation of human intelligence in machines, enabling them to learn, reason, and make decisions.

DS Dependency:

  • Training on Distributed GPU Clusters — multiple GPUs across servers process huge datasets faster
  • Cloud Storage — AI needs enormous amounts of data stored in cloud databases
  • Massive Data Processing — distributed computing allows simultaneous processing

Without distributed systems, AI cannot scale. e.g. image recognition, chatbots.

⛓️
Blockchain

Definition: A decentralized, peer-to-peer ledger system that records transactions in a secure, immutable way.

DS Dependency:

  • P2P Networks — every node stores a copy of the ledger and validates transactions
  • Distributed Consensus — PoW or PBFT ensures all nodes agree on the correct state
  • No Central Authority — operates entirely on distributed nodes
🥽
Virtual Reality (VR)

Definition: Creates immersive, computer-generated 3D environments that users can interact with in real time.

DS Dependency:

  • Low-Latency Networks — any delay causes motion sickness
  • High Bandwidth — large amounts of graphics, video, and positional data
  • Cloud-Based Rendering — offloads computation to distributed servers
🚁
Drones

Definition: Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) capable of autonomous or remote-controlled flight.

DS Dependency:

  • Cloud Communication — send real-time data to cloud platforms for analysis
  • GPS and Real-Time Data — navigation requires global positioning and live sensor data
  • Distributed Control Systems — drone fleets use swarm robotics requiring distributed coordination
⌚
Wearable Technology

Definition: Devices worn on the body — smartwatches, fitness trackers, health monitors.

DS Dependency:

  • Sensor Data Collection — continuously collect heart rate, steps, temperature
  • Sync with Smartphones — devices upload data in real time
  • Cloud Storage and Processing — data analyzed in distributed cloud systems for health tracking and AI insights

Question 1 — 25 Marks (COMPULSORY)
1(a). Centralized, Decentralized, Distributed Network, Embedded System & Network Topology 5 marks ▼
TermDefinition
Centralized NetworkA network where all nodes connect to and depend on a single central server or controller. All communication passes through this central point. If it fails, the entire network fails. Example: Old mainframe systems.
Decentralized NetworkA network where there are multiple control points but not fully distributed. Authority is spread across several nodes rather than one. Example: Banking network with regional servers.
Distributed NetworkA network where processing and control are spread across all nodes. No single point of control. Each node can function independently. Example: The Internet, Blockchain.
Embedded SystemA computer system with a dedicated function built into a larger mechanical or electrical system. It has fixed hardware and software designed for specific tasks. Example: Microcontroller in a microwave oven, ABS system in a car.
Network TopologyThe arrangement/layout of the nodes and connections in a network. It describes how devices are physically or logically connected. Types: Bus, Star, Ring, Mesh, Tree topology.
1(b). Three (3) Key Driver/Motivators for Net-Centric Computing (Daniel, 1921) 3 marks ▼

Daniel (1921) highlighted the following key drivers/motivators for the innovative use of Net-Centric Computing:

  1. Increased Connectivity — The rapid growth of internet access and mobile networks made it possible for more devices and people to be connected. This connectivity is the foundation that makes Net-Centric Computing viable and scalable.
  2. Cost Reduction — Moving computing to the network reduces the need for expensive local hardware and software. Organizations can share resources, reducing infrastructure cost significantly.
  3. Scalability and Flexibility — Net-Centric systems can scale up or down on demand without physical hardware changes, allowing organizations to adapt quickly to business needs.
1(c). Characteristics of Embedded System (Performance & Functional Requirements) 3 marks ▼

Performance Requirements:

  • Real-Time Operation — Must respond to inputs within defined time constraints (e.g., airbag must deploy within milliseconds)
  • Power Efficiency — Designed to operate on minimal power, especially for battery-powered devices
  • Reliability — Must operate continuously without failure for long periods

Functional Requirements:

  • Dedicated Functionality — Designed for one specific task only (unlike a general-purpose computer)
  • Hardware-Software Integration — The software is tightly coupled with specific hardware components
  • Compact Size — Must fit within the physical constraints of the device it is embedded in

Question 2 — 15 Marks
2(a). Grid Computing, Cloud Computing & Sensor Network 5 marks each ▼

(i) Grid Computing

Grid Computing is a distributed computing model that coordinates resources across multiple administrative domains to solve large-scale problems. It connects geographically dispersed computers (which may belong to different organizations) to work together on a single task as if they were a single supercomputer.

🗣️ Real-Life Example
SETI@home — millions of home computers donated spare processing power to analyze radio telescope data for signs of extraterrestrial intelligence. No single computer could handle the data alone.

Key Features: Resource sharing across organizations, Dynamic provisioning, Heterogeneous systems work together, Typically used for scientific/research computing.

(ii) Cloud Computing

Cloud Computing is a technology that allows access and use of computing resources (servers, storage, databases, networking, software) over the internet instead of owning and managing them locally. The cloud implementation of service orientation is referred to as XaaS (Everything as a Service).

💡 Real-Life Example
Google Drive — instead of saving files on your hard disk, they are stored on Google's servers and accessible from any device with internet.

Key Features: On-demand self-service, Broad network access, Resource pooling (multi-tenancy), Rapid elasticity, Measured service (pay-as-you-go).

(iii) Sensor Network

A Sensor Network (or Wireless Sensor Network — WSN) is a collection of spatially distributed autonomous sensors that monitor physical or environmental conditions (temperature, humidity, pressure, motion) and cooperatively pass data through the network to a main location.

✅ Real-Life Example
Smart agriculture — sensors in a farm field continuously monitor soil moisture, temperature, and rainfall, sending data to a central server that controls irrigation automatically.

Key Features: Self-organizing, Energy-constrained (battery-powered), Wireless communication, Data aggregation, Used in IoT/smart environments.

Question 3 — 15 Marks
3(a). Middleware Frameworks: RPC, RMI & Web Services/Message-Oriented Middleware 5 marks each ▼

(i) Remote Procedure Call (RPC)

RPC is a protocol that allows a program on one computer to execute a procedure (function/subroutine) on another computer over a network as if it were a local call. The caller does not need to know about the network details — the middleware handles communication transparently.

How it works:

  1. Client calls a local stub (proxy) function
  2. Stub packages the parameters (marshalling) and sends them over the network
  3. Server-side stub receives and unpacks (unmarshalling) the parameters
  4. Server executes the procedure and sends the result back
  5. Client receives the result as if the function ran locally
🗣️ Plain English
You call your friend and ask them to look up something in their textbook and tell you the answer. You made the "call" from your location; they did the "procedure" from theirs. That's RPC.

(ii) Remote Method Invocation (RMI)

RMI is the Java-based implementation of RPC for object-oriented systems. It allows a Java program to invoke methods on objects that exist on a remote Java Virtual Machine (JVM) over a network. Unlike RPC which works with procedures, RMI works with entire objects.

How it works:

  1. Remote objects are registered in an RMI Registry (naming service)
  2. Client looks up the remote object in the registry
  3. Client gets a stub (local proxy) for the remote object
  4. Client invokes methods on the stub — the skeleton on the server receives and executes them
  5. Results are serialized and returned to the client
💡 RMI vs RPC
RPC = procedure-level (language neutral). RMI = object-level (Java-specific). RMI supports object-oriented concepts like inheritance and polymorphism over the network.

(iii) Web Services / Message-Oriented Middleware (MOM)

Web Services are software components that communicate over the internet using standard protocols (HTTP, SOAP, REST). They allow applications built on different platforms and languages to exchange data and functionality.

Message-Oriented Middleware (MOM) provides asynchronous communication between distributed systems through a message queue. Instead of direct calls, systems send and receive messages from queues, decoupling the sender and receiver.

How Web Services work:

  1. Service is published/registered (UDDI registry)
  2. Client discovers the service
  3. Client invokes via SOAP (XML-based) or REST (HTTP-based) protocol
  4. Server processes and returns response in XML/JSON
✅ Real-Life Example
When you book a flight on a travel website, it calls the airline's Web Service API to check availability and pricing in real time — even though both systems are built differently.
Question 4 — 15 Marks
4(a). Basic Security Vulnerabilities of Mobile Computing 5 marks ▼
  • Device Theft/Loss — Mobile devices are small and easily lost or stolen, giving physical access to stored data
  • Insecure Wi-Fi — Connecting to public/unencrypted Wi-Fi exposes data to eavesdropping (Man-in-the-Middle attacks)
  • Malware & Malicious Apps — Downloading apps from unofficial sources can install spyware, ransomware, or trojans
  • Weak Authentication — Simple PINs or no screen locks allow unauthorized physical access
  • Outdated OS/Apps — Unpatched security vulnerabilities in older software versions can be exploited
  • Data Leakage via Apps — Apps may request excessive permissions and transmit personal data without user consent

Question 5 — 15 Marks
5(a). Three Distributed System Models + Architectural Model 10 marks ▼

The Three Distributed System Models:

  1. Client-Server Model — One computer (server) provides data/services; others (clients) request them. Foundation of most Internet systems.
  2. Peer-to-Peer (P2P) Model — All nodes are equal; each can be both client and server. No central authority. Examples: BitTorrent, Blockchain.
  3. Three-Tier / N-Tier Model — Extends client-server with additional middle layers handling logic, separating presentation, business logic, and data storage.

The Architectural Model in Detail:

The Architectural Model defines who communicates with whom in a distributed system. It specifies:

  • How system components are structured and organized
  • Where computation happens (client-side, server-side, or distributed)
  • How communication occurs between components
  • How data is stored and accessed across the system
  • How scalability, reliability, fault tolerance, and security are achieved

The two foundational architectural models are Client-Server (centralized control, one server serves many clients) and Peer-to-Peer (decentralized, every node shares responsibility).

Question 6 — 15 Marks
6(i). Middleware in Mobile Computing 5 marks ▼

Middleware in Mobile Computing refers to the software layer that sits between the mobile application and the underlying network/operating system infrastructure. It provides a set of common services and abstractions that allow mobile applications to communicate with backend systems, other devices, and services across heterogeneous networks.

In mobile computing, middleware addresses challenges such as:

  • Network disconnection and reconnection (intermittent connectivity)
  • Limited bandwidth and battery power
  • Device heterogeneity (different OS, screen sizes, capabilities)
  • Location awareness — tracking and responding to device position
🗣️ Plain English
When your banking app on your phone talks to the bank's server, it doesn't know or care whether you're on Wi-Fi or 4G, or what server the bank is running. The middleware in the middle handles all those differences so the app just works.
